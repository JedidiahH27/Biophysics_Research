This study investigates the potential of antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) as alternatives to combat antibiotic resistance, with a focus on two AMPs containing unnatural amino acids (UAAs), E2-53R (16 AAs) and LE-54R (14 AAs). In both peptides, valine is replaced by norvaline (Nva), and tryptophan is replaced by 1,2,3,4-Tetrahydroisoquinoline-3-carboxylic acid (Tic).

Microbiological studies reveal potent activity against both Gram-negative (Gâˆ’) and Gram-positive (G+) bacteria without toxicity to eukaryotic cells. Circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy shows that both peptides maintain Î±-helical structures when interacting with G(âˆ’) and G(+) lipid model membranes (LMMs), a characteristic associated with their antimicrobial efficacy.

X-ray diffuse scattering (XDS) demonstrates membrane softening and reduced chain order in G(âˆ’), G(+), and eukaryotic (Euk33) LMMs, suggesting membrane destabilization as a potential mechanism. Furthermore, XDS identifies a significant correlation between peptide interfacial location and efficacy. Neutron reflectometry (NR) confirms the AMP locations determined via XDS. These UAAs-based AMPs offer a promising strategy to fight resistant bacteria while preserving host cell integrity. Comparisons are made with a previously studied natural peptide, E2-35, containing arginine, tryptophan, and valine.
